OVERVIEW

The X-Camp Student Support System

When students get stuck on homework, they have two ways to get help.

🌙

Homework Night (HWN)

Every day, 5:00–8:00 PM PT. Students post on Discourse and ask questions by voice in Zoom. Multiple TAs handle volume in real time. Fast-paced, requires coordination.

🗓

1 on 1 Office Hours

Students book individual sessions in advance — Conceptual, Debugging, Code Review, or Catch-up. Slower-paced, deeper focus, requires TA preparation.

The two channels complement each other — HWN handles most common questions, and 1 on 1 OH handles situations that need deeper, focused work.

💡 As a TA, your core responsibility is not to give answers. It is to help students find their own path to solving the problem.
🎯
SECTION A · A1

Your Goal

The goal of HWN is not to answer every question completely. It is to make sure every student who comes for help leaves with a clear next step.

That means

🔍A student with a WA knows exactly which part of their logic to re-examine
💡A student with no idea at all has one concrete direction to try
🔀A student out of your coverage has been handed off cleanly to the right TA

You do not need to fully resolve every problem. Guiding students to the right direction and handing off cleanly is doing your job well.

Roles in HWN

Each HWN shift has three roles. You will be assigned to one of them. Click a role to see its responsibilities.

💬 Discourse Teacher (Co-Host)

🎙 Zoom Teacher — you coordinate the entire room

  • Claim Host as soon as you enter Zoom (Host Key is in the HWN Guidebook)
  • Enable "Play sound when someone joins or leaves" under Manage Participants
  • Dispatch questions in order of arrival:
    • Can handle it → reply "Looking" and address directly by voice or Discourse
    • Level mismatch → @ the correct Discourse TA
    • No coverage → "Your post will be replied within 24 hours"
  • Keep Discourse post tags up to date
⚠️ When a student posts a Zoom chat message with their Discourse link, always go to the Discourse post to handle it — don't just respond in Zoom chat.

💬 Discourse Teacher — you focus on written Q&A

  • Prioritize questions the Zoom Teacher @ tags you on — reply "Looking" in Zoom and take over
  • Then check new Discourse notifications
  • Then answer posts in chronological order
  • Update the post tag after every reply

SECTION A · A4

How to Handle a Question

Step 1 — Identify the question type

TypeWhat it looks likeYour first move
Don't understandCan't follow the problem statement"What specifically don't you understand — the description, or the sample data?"
No ideaNo approach at all"How long have you been thinking about it?"
ImplementationHas a plan but can't write it"What have you tried? Where exactly are you stuck?"
CECompile error"Have you looked at the compiler's error message?"
WAWrong answer"Have you found the test case where your output is wrong?"
RERuntime error"Do you know where in the code the program crashes?"
TLETime limit exceeded"What algorithm are you currently using?"
MLEMemory limit exceeded"What data structure are you using to store the data?"
💡 Core principle: ask first, guide second, give direction last. Jumping straight to giving the answer takes away the student's chance to figure it out themselves — and they'll be stuck again next time.

Step 2 — Use rounds when multiple students are waiting

Round 1
Give every student a quick reply ("Looking" + brief directional nudge)
Round 2
Return to each student based on their progress, give more detailed guidance
Round 3+
Continue following up on complex problems

This way, no student is left waiting in silence for a long time.

Step 3 — Decide whether to hand off

If a question is clearly outside your level coverage, don't force it. @ the correct Discourse TA and let the student know in Zoom chat that the right TA is looking at their post.

SECTION A · A5

Language Reference & Case Studies

Useful Phrases

Copy these directly or adapt them to your style.

When a student's code is too complex to follow:
"There are many ways to solve this and I didn't use the same approach. It would really help if you could write out your solution plan in English first — what is each part of your code trying to do?"
When a student says they have no idea:

Ask how long they've been thinking about it first. Then give the smallest useful nudge based on the problem type (e.g. "This is a tree problem — start by thinking about how to build the tree"), not the full solution.

When you can't cover the question right now:
"Your post will be replied within 24 hours. You're welcome to stay for self-study, or come back tomorrow."

Don't pretend to be handling it and give a vague direction. Being honest is better than a misleading answer.

Case Studies

Click a case to expand it.

1
The student posted code but you can't follow their logic ▼

Amy posted a 200-level DP problem on Discourse. She pasted 60 lines of code with no comments and said "my code is WA."

❌ Common mistake
You spend 10 minutes trying to read through her code, then give a hint based on your own solution approach — which has nothing to do with what she wrote. Amy gets more confused.
✅ What to do
  1. Reply "Looking" and change the tag to Replied
  2. Send: "There are many ways to solve this. Could you write out your solution logic in English first? What is each section of your code trying to do?"
  3. Once she explains her approach, you can see exactly where her thinking went wrong and guide her from there.
2
Zoom Teacher and Discourse Teacher both pick up the same question ▼

Ben sends his Discourse link in Zoom chat. At the same time, the Discourse TA sees the new notification and starts looking at it too.

❌ Common mistake
Both TAs respond with different directions. Ben doesn't know which one to follow.
✅ What to do
  1. Zoom Teacher sees Ben is 200-level, outside their coverage, and @ the Discourse TA
  2. Discourse TA replies "Looking" in Zoom and takes over
  3. Zoom Teacher tells Ben: "Our 200-level TA is looking at your post now"

Only one TA handles any given question at a time.

3
The question is clearly above your level coverage ▼

Carol is a 201-level student, but the problem she's asking about is clearly 300-level material.

❌ Common mistake
You try to answer anyway, give an inaccurate hint, and Carol spends an hour going in the wrong direction.
✅ What to do
  1. Don't attempt an answer you're not confident in
  2. Tell Carol: "This problem looks like it's from the 300-level curriculum. I'll flag it for our team — someone will follow up within 24 hours."
  3. Let the other TAs or Manager know in the group so the right person can follow up
4
A student keeps asking follow-ups without trying anything ▼

David asks a question. You give him a nudge. He immediately asks "so what do I do next?" — without having tried anything.

❌ Common mistake
You keep answering, and by the end you've essentially walked him through the entire solution.
✅ What to do
  1. After the second follow-up without any attempt, pause: "Before I continue — what have you tried based on what I just said?"
  2. If he hasn't tried anything: "Go ahead and try implementing that part first. Come back if you're still stuck — I'll be here."
  3. Don't let student impatience push you into skipping the guided learning process.
5
You're the only TA on shift with too many questions ▼

One Wednesday evening, another TA is absent and you're handling 8 students alone.

✅ What to do
  1. Don't try to fully resolve any one question first — use rounds, give every student a quick "Looking" + one-line nudge
  2. For deeper debugging: "This will take more time — I'll come back to you after checking in with everyone."
  3. For questions you can't cover: "Your post will be replied within 24 hours." Leave a note in the TA group.
  4. After the shift, flag to the Manager that coverage was thin.
✅

SECTION B · B1

Your Goal

1 on 1 OH is a scheduled, one-on-one session between you and a single student. Unlike HWN where you're managing multiple students at once, here you have the student's full attention — and they have yours.

That higher level of focus comes with a higher standard. Students book a session because they're stuck on something specific. Your job is to make sure they leave with a real understanding of what was wrong and how to think about it next time — not just a fixed piece of code.

The four session types

Session typeDurationWhat the student needs
💡 Conceptual Session25 minHelp understanding the approach or algorithm
🐛 Debugging Session15 minHelp finding and fixing a specific bug
🔍 Code Review20 minFeedback on accepted code — style, efficiency, edge cases
📅 Catch-up Session30 minHelp getting back on track after falling behind
📌 400-level and Candidate Master TAs: The session types and how you run them are the same. The Zoom setup has minor differences — see the note in B2.

💡 Conceptual Session — 25 min

The student doesn't understand the approach. Help them arrive at the right mental model themselves.

Suggested Flow

0–3 minAsk the student to explain what they understand so far in their own words
3–10 minIdentify the exact gap — is it the concept itself, or how to apply it to this problem?
10–20 minGuide them toward the insight using questions, not explanations
20–25 minHave them summarize the approach back to you; confirm their next step

Useful Questions

  • "What does the problem actually ask you to do — can you say it in one sentence?"
  • "Have you seen a simpler version of this problem before?"
  • "If you solved this by hand for the sample input, what would you do step by step?"
  • "What's the smallest subproblem you'd need to solve first?"
⚠️ What to avoid: Explaining the algorithm directly in the first 10 minutes. If you explain before the student has had a chance to think, they'll follow along in the moment but won't retain it.

🐛 Debugging Session — 15 min

The student has code that isn't working. Help them find and understand the bug — don't just identify it for them.

Suggested Flow

0–2 minAsk the student to describe the bug and show you the failing case
2–8 minReproduce the error together — read the code, trace through the logic
8–13 minGuide them to the bug using questions; let them make the fix
13–15 minConfirm the fix works; ask why the original code was wrong

Useful Questions

  • "Can you walk me through what your code does, line by line, for this input?"
  • "At which line do you think the value becomes wrong?"
  • "What did you expect this variable to be at this point? What is it actually?"
  • "If you comment out this section, does the error change?"
⚠️ What to avoid: Reading through their code silently and then telling them what's wrong. The student needs to be the one tracing through the logic.

🔍 Code Review — 20 min

The student's code is accepted. Give specific, actionable feedback on the dimensions they asked about.

Suggested Flow

0–3 minAsk what specific feedback they want — don't assume based on the booking form
3–15 minReview the code together against their stated goals
15–20 minSummarize 2–3 concrete takeaways they can apply going forward

Things Worth Commenting On

  • Time and space complexity — is there a more efficient approach?
  • Edge cases — empty input, single elements, integer overflow?
  • Readability — are variable names clear? Is the logic easy to follow?
  • Structure — unnecessary repetition? Could anything be extracted into a function?
⚠️ What to avoid: Rewriting the code for them, or giving so much feedback that nothing sticks. Pick the 2–3 most important things and go deep on those.

📅 Catch-up Session — 30 min

The student has fallen behind. This session is as much about clarity and momentum as it is about content.

Suggested Flow

0–5 minAsk the student to explain what they've missed and why — no judgment, just understanding
5–15 minIdentify the one concept gap that's most blocking their progress
15–25 minWork through that concept together
25–30 minSet a specific, achievable goal for the next week

Things to Keep in Mind

  • Don't try to catch them up on everything in one session. One concept done well is better than three concepts rushed.
  • End every Catch-up session with a concrete next action: "By next week, complete problems X and Y and come back if you're stuck."
  • If the student is behind due to external circumstances (illness, time zone issues), flag this to the Class Coordinator after the session — don't try to manage it yourself.

Case Studies

Click a case to expand it.

1
The student expects you to walk them through the solution ▼

Eric books a Conceptual Session for a DP problem. He opens the session by saying "I have no idea — can you just explain how to solve it?"

❌ Common mistake
You explain the approach from start to finish. Eric follows along, says "I get it now," and books another session next week for the same reason.
✅ What to do
  1. Acknowledge the feeling: "Totally fine — let's figure out where to start together."
  2. Redirect: "Before I explain anything, can you tell me what the problem is asking in your own words?"
  3. Build from what he does know, using questions. Even "I have no idea" students usually understand more than they think once you start asking.
  4. If he genuinely can't engage, check whether he's watched the hint video — that's the right first step, not your explanation.
2
The student's bug is obvious to you but you fix it too quickly ▼

Fiona books a Debugging Session. You glance at her code and immediately spot an off-by-one error in her loop.

❌ Common mistake
You say "Line 12 — your loop condition should be < n not <= n." Fiona fixes it, the code passes, and she leaves having learned nothing about how to find bugs herself.
✅ What to do
  1. Don't point to the line directly. Instead: "Can you trace through what happens when the input is just one element?"
  2. Let her trace through it herself until she reaches the wrong value.
  3. Once she sees the wrong value: "So what should this have been? Where does that come from?"
  4. She finds the bug. The fix takes 10 seconds — the understanding takes 10 minutes and is worth it.
3
The student brings a problem clearly above their level ▼

George is in the 201-level class and books a Conceptual Session for a problem that's clearly 300-level material.

✅ What to do
  1. Don't attempt a full session on material outside your confidence range.
  2. In the session: "This is a great problem — it's actually from the 300-level curriculum. Let me flag it for your coordinator so you get the right guidance."
  3. Tag the session above-level question in Zoom after.
  4. After 2 flags for the same student, the system auto-alerts management — you don't need to do anything further.
4
The student no-shows ▼

You're in the Zoom room at the scheduled time. Five minutes pass and the student hasn't joined.

✅ What to do

Follow the no-show protocol in the setup guide exactly.
